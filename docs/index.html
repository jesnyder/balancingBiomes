Balancing Biomes

The objective is to review peer reviewed research to determine if soil microbes protect plants from saltwater infiltration. And if so, which plants. This knowledge may guide practices to balance the biomes of salt water infiltrated land to protect native species or yields from agricultural lands.

Considerations:

  • Comparison of two plants (one salt tolerant and the other not) identified a battery of genes that responded to high salt conditions. This suggests halophytes have an internal system to identify and respond to high salt and soil microbes do not play a part. https://doi.org/10.1104/pp.104.039909
  • Salt cress detects salt stress and adapts its stromata (gas pores), adds a layer to leaves, and root structure. "Stomata [a pore allowing gas exchange] of salt cress are distributed on the leaf surface at higher density, but are less open than the stomata of Arabidopsis and respond to salt stress by closing more tightly." https://doi.org/10.1104/pp.104.041723
  • "These studies illustrate that halophilic PGPRs from the rhizosphere of halophytic species can be effective bio-inoculants for promoting the production of non-halophytic species in saline soils." https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2018.00148/full
  • Halophytes hold promise to meet food needs for 2050 and for salt remediation. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.envexpbot.2014.05.006
  • "certain halotolerant bacteria, isolated from coastal soils, have a real potential to enhance plant growth under saline stress, through the reduction of ethylene production via ACC deaminase activity." https://www.dbpia.co.kr/Journal/articleDetail?nodeId=NODE09076651
  • A plant from an arid environment with higher polyphenol production and antioxidant activity was more resistant to salt stress. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.plaphy.2007.02.001
  • "We conclude that in salt-stressed Thellungiella, plastid terminal oxidase acts as an alternative electron sink, accounting for up to 30% of total PSII electron flow." https://doi.org/10.1104/pp.108.132407
  • "we discuss the possible implications of these bacterial mechanisms on the plant growth promotion or homeostasis regulation in natural conditions." https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00253-009-2116-3
